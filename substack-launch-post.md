# The Six Dimensions That Determine Whether Your Business Is Built to Last — or Built to Break

Most businesses don't fail because the product is bad. They fail because the infrastructure underneath the product was never designed to scale.

I spent years working inside companies as an operator — restructuring teams, rebuilding financial controls, ripping out software stacks that were costing six figures in hidden drag. The pattern is always the same: the founder built something that works, but they built it around themselves. And at some point, that stops working.

The businesses that survive the transition from "founder-run" to "actually valuable" all share the same structural advantage. They got the foundation right across six dimensions before they needed to.

Here's the framework I use to diagnose where a business actually stands.

---

## 1. Personnel & Org

The question that matters most: *If you disappeared for 30 days, would the business keep running?*

Owner dependency is the single most common structural deficiency I see. It doesn't show up on a P&L. It doesn't trigger an alarm. It just silently caps your enterprise value and makes you unsellable.

The fix isn't "hiring more people." It's building a leadership layer that can make decisions without you. If more than half your leadership team wouldn't be considered A-players by a top competitor in your industry, you don't have a leadership team — you have expensive task-doers.

## 2. Accounting & Finance

Revenue is not the same as financial health. I've seen companies doing $10M+ that couldn't produce a clean cash flow statement in under a week.

The structural test: Can you produce financials within 15 business days of month-end? Do you actually run a formal budget process, or are you flying on gut? Is your accounting function a strategic asset — forecasting, scenario-planning, informing decisions — or is it just a compliance cost?

If your CFO (or bookkeeper, or whoever owns this) is primarily doing data entry, you're paying for accounting but getting none of its value.

## 3. Software Stack

Most mid-market businesses are running 3-5 redundant tools they're paying for monthly and barely using. That's not the real cost. The real cost is the manual workarounds your team invented because nothing talks to each other.

The structural test: How much time does your team spend on manual data transfers between systems? Is your CRM actually the system of record, or do people keep their own spreadsheets? When was the last time someone audited whether you're getting ROI from your tools — not just whether people are logging in?

## 4. AI Readiness

This is the dimension where I see the widest gap between perception and reality. Most founders think AI readiness means "we use ChatGPT sometimes." That's not readiness. That's tourism.

AI readiness means: your data is structured and accessible, you've mapped your highest-value repetitive workflows, you have a realistic dollar estimate of what automation would save, and you have someone accountable for evaluating and deploying AI tools — not just experimenting with them on a Tuesday afternoon.

## 5. Sales & Marketing

A business with strong revenue but no documented sales process is a business that got lucky and doesn't know it yet.

The structural test: Could a new hire replicate your top performer's results using your documented playbook? Do you know your actual cost-per-lead and conversion rates by channel — not estimates, but numbers? Is your pipeline managed in a real system with stages and probabilities, or is it a spreadsheet that one person updates when they remember?

The companies that scale sales profitably all have the same boring trait: they measured everything before they spent more.

## 6. Operations & Process

This is where the "we've always done it this way" tax lives. Every undocumented process is a single point of failure disguised as institutional knowledge.

The structural test: Are your top 10 workflows documented well enough that a competent new hire could execute them in week one? Do you have formal SLAs or quality benchmarks — and do you actually track against them? When something breaks, is there a post-mortem process, or does the same failure just happen again in six months?
